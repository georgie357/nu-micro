Life as we know it is based on carbon
4 most common elements in living matter: C, N, O, and H
A carbon atom can form 4 covalent bonds, allowing it to build large and diverse organic compounds
Hydrocarbons only have C and H
Are energy rich & nonpolar
Most biomolecules contain functional groups

Different bonding sequence: structural isomers

--- SLIDE 8 ---
Functional groups
Distinctive properties of organic molecules depend on the carbon skeleton and on the chemical groups attached to it
A functional group is a group of atoms bonded to a carbon of an organic compound giving a property (polarity, acidity etc), ex.-COOH (acid), -OH (alcohol)

--- SLIDE 17 ---
Monomers and polymers
Most biomolecules are built of smaller subunits (monomers)
Polymers contain many monomers
Examples: monosaccharides/polysaccharides; amino acids/proteins, nucleotides/nucleic acids
Dehydration reaction builds polymers from monomers, hydrolysis breaks them down
--- SLIDE 18 ---
Hydrolysis and dehydration
Adding water breaks polymers to monomers
Removal of water makes polymers out of monomers

Phospholipids and Biological Membranes
Phospholipids – modified triglycerides with two fatty acid groups (apolar, hydrophobic) and a phosphorus group (polar, hydrophilic))
Structure allows to interact with BOTH water & fats => lipid bilayers in cell membranes

--- SLIDE 34 ---
Proteins (polypeptides)
Contain C, H, N, and O, some also S and P
Monomers: amino acids
20 amino acids
Can perform a variety of functions, such as catalysis (enzymes), transport, structure, communication (receptors, hormones), defense (antibodies, clotting factors) etc.
The sequence of amino acids is the information coded in the genes
--- SLIDE 35 ---
Amino Acids
20 (plus some extras), contain an amino group and a carboxyl group
Amino group NH2; Carboxyl group COOH
Side chain (R) can be polar, non-polar, charged.
--- SLIDE 36 ---
Peptide bond
The –NH2 group of one amino acid can bind to the –COOH group of another => peptide bond (dehydration reaction)
Less than 50 amino acids= peptides
Many amino acids bound by peptide bonds = polypeptide (aka protein)
--- SLIDE 37 ---
Protein Structure
Primary – amino acid sequence
Secondary – alpha helices or beta pleated sheets
Tertiary – superimposed folding of secondary structures (H-bonds, disulfide bridges, van der Waals)
Quaternary – polypeptide chains linked together in a specific manner
Non-protein groups: lipo- and glycoproteins
ALL together determine the structure of the protein, which is NECESSARY for its function.
