Robert Remak: cells arise from other cells via cell division
Rudolf Virchow: omnis cellula a cellula (“all cells arise from cells”), basic tenet of cell theory

What causes diseases? Miasma per ancient Greeks, see—like spores per Francastoro (1500s).
Ignaz Semmelweiss (1847): observed connection between puerperal fever and no handwashing

Prokaryotic Cells
Prokaryotic cells do not contain internal membrane bound organelles
They have plasma membrane and ribosomes
They have a region called nucleoid where DNA is (NOT nucleus)
Almost all have a cell wall also.
Size is 10 X smaller (higher surface-to-volume ratio)- grow faster than eukaryotes

Endospores

--- Slide 22 ---
Sporulation
Generally occurs when a key nutrient such as carbon or nitrogen becomes scarce or unavailable
A spore is formed inside the bacterial cell.
Can be in the middle or to one end.
DNA is packaged inside.
When done, the vegetative cell disintegrates, and the endospore is free.
--- Slide 23 ---
Germination
A spore can return to being a vegetative cell by a process called germination
Triggered by physical or chemical damage to the spore coat
Forms a single cell
Endospore formation is…
Not a reproduction method
A survival system

--- Slide 27 ---
Cell Wall
Semi-rigid structure, adds shape, protects cell from rupture when water pressure inside cell is too high
Point of anchor for flagella
Clinically important because it contributes to ability of some organisms to cause disease
Site of action of some antibiotics
--- Slide 28 ---
Chemical composition of cell wall
Composed of peptidoglycan (in bacteria) either alone or in combination with other substances
Repeating disaccharide composed of N-acetylglucosamine (NAG) and N-acetylmuramic acid (NAM), attached by polypeptides

--- Slide 32 ---
Gram staining and the cell wall
Primary Stain = crystal violet
Stains both gram positive and gram negative cells purple because the dye enters the cytoplasm of both types of cells
Mordant = Gram’s  iodine
Crystal violet-iodine (CV-I) crystals from; too large to escape through cell wall
Destain = alcohol
Gram+: Alcohol dehydrates the peptidoglycan, CV-I does not leave
Gram -: Alcohol dissolves outer membrane; leaves small holes in the peptidoglycan layer; CV-I can diffuse out of cell
Counterstain = safranin
Gm- cells colorless after the alcohol wash, safranin stains them pink/red

--- Slide 51 ---
Microfilaments
A microfilament is composed of a pair of actin filaments.
Each actin filament is a string of polymerized actin monomers.
The dynamic nature of actin, due to its polymerization and depolymerization and its association with myosin, allows microfilaments to be involved in a variety of cellular processes, including ameboid movement, cytoplasmic streaming, contractile ring formation during cell division, and muscle contraction in animals.
--- Slide 52 ---
Intermediate filaments
Intermediate filaments are composed of multiple strands of polymerized subunits. They are more permanent than other cytoskeletal structures and serve a variety of functions.
Intermediate filaments form much of the nuclear lamina.
Intermediate filaments form the desmosomes between cells in some animal tissues. (credit c “illustration”: modification of work by Mariana Ruiz Villareal)

Cell wall and extracellular matrix
Fungi, plants, algae, and some protists have cell walls
Different from prokaryotes
