--- SLIDE 3 ---
Relative sizes of microorganisms
Typical virus measures about 100 nm, 10 times smaller than a typical bacterium (~1 µm), which is at least 10 times smaller than a typical plant or animal cell (~10–100 µm).
An object must measure about 100 µm to be visible without a microscope.
--- SLIDE 4 ---
Properties of waves
Wavelength: distance between one peak of a wave and the next peak.
Amplitude: height of each peak (or depth of each trough)
Frequency: rate of vibration of the wave, or the number of wavelengths within a specified time period
--- SLIDE 5 ---
Properties of light
Reflection: wave bounces off of a material.
Absorbance: material captures the energy of a light wave.
Transmission: wave travels through a material
Transparency vs opacity

--- SLIDE 6 ---
Refraction
The refractive index is a measure of the light-bending ability of a medium
The light may bend in air so much that it misses the small high-magnification lens
Immersion oil is used to keep light from bending
--- SLIDE 7 ---
Electromagnetic Spectrum and Color
Visible light is a narrow range of electromagnetic radiations
Longer wavelength= less energy
Shorter wavelength= higher energy
(credit: modification of work by Johannes Ahlmann)
--- SLIDE 8 ---
Magnification, Resolution, and Contrast
Magnification: ability of a lens to enlarge the image of an object when compared to the real object.
Resolution is the ability of the lenses to distinguish two points
A microscope with a resolving power of 0.4 nm can distinguish between two points ≥ 0.4 nm
Shorter wavelengths of light provide greater resolution
NA- numerical aperture, the higher=> the better the resolution

--- SLIDE 15 ---
Electron Microscopy
Uses electrons instead of light
The shorter wavelength of electrons gives greater resolution
Can be transmission or scanning
--- SLIDE 16 ---
Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)
Ultrathin sections of specimens
Light passes through specimen, then an electromagnetic lens, to a screen or film
Specimens may be stained with heavy metal salts
10,000–100,000; resolution 2.5 nm
--- SLIDE 17 ---
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
An electron gun produces a beam of electrons that scans the surface of a whole specimen
Secondary electrons emitted from the specimen produce the image
1,000–10,000; resolution 20 nm

--- SLIDE 22 ---
Staining
Staining: Coloring the microbe with a dye that emphasizes certain structures
Stains are salts composed of a positive and a negative ion, one of which is colored (chromophore)
Basic dyes: color is in the positive ion
Acidic dyes: color is in the negative ion
Negatively charged bacteria will attract positively charged (basic) dyes
Crystal violet, methylene blue, malachite green, safranin
--- SLIDE 23 ---
Simple stain
Only one stain is used, all cells are stained
Aqueous or alcohol solution of a single, basic dye
Stain is applied to fixed smear and then washed off
(credit a: modification of work by Centers for Disease Control and Prevention; credit b: modification of work by Roberto Danovaro, Antonio Pusceddu, Cristina Gambi, Iben Heiner, Reinhardt Mobjerg Kristensen; credit c: modification of work by Anh-Hue Tu)
--- SLIDE 24 ---
Use of simple stains
Determine the shape of the cells (i.e., cocci, bacilli, spirilla)
Look at the arrangement of the cells
Singles, pairs, tetrads, etc.
Estimate size of cells
Can use ocular micrometer if available
Estimate size based on field of view diameter
--- SLIDE 25 ---
Differential stains
React differently with different kinds of bacteria and can be used to distinguish among them
Sometimes a special treatment/chemical is used to “set” the stain => mordant
Gram stain is most frequently used differential stain: Gram + and Gram - bacteria
Acid fast stain
--- SLIDE 26 ---
Gram staining
Developed in 1884 by Hans Christian Gram
Differentiates bacteria into two large groups
Gram positive and Gram negative
Based on difference in cell wall structure
All cells are stained with crystal violet
Destained with alcohol: Gram + stay purple, Gram – lose color
Counterstained with safranin (pink)

Acid Fast Staining
Mycobacterium, Nocardia
Have a waxy cell wall, difficult to destain
Cells are stained with carbofuschin (pink) & decolorized with acid-alcohol
Only acid fast cells stay pink, rest counterstained with blue
